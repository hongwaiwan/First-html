Einstein's 1905 derivation of $E=mc^2$ — written in modern notation

This page reproduces the thought experiment and algebra Einstein used in his short 1905 paper "Does the inertia of a body depend upon its energy content?" and expresses the steps in today's notation.

Setup (thought experiment)
  1. A body is initially at rest in inertial frame $S$.
  2. The body emits two identical pulses of light in opposite directions; each pulse carries energy $L/2$, so the total emitted light energy (in frame $S$) is $L$.
  3. Because the pulses are equal and opposite, the body remains at rest in $S$ (no net recoil).

Step 1 — Conservation of energy in the rest frame $S$

Let the body's energy before emission be $E_0$ and after emission $E_1$. Conservation of energy in $S$ gives

$E_0 = E_1 + L$.

Step 2 — Look from a frame $S'$ moving with velocity $v$ relative to $S$

Denote energies measured in $S'$ by capital letters with a prime: before emission $H_0$, after emission $H_1$ and the total energy of the two light pulses in $S'$ is $L'$. Because energy is conserved in $S'$,

$H_0 = H_1 + L'.$

We need how the light energy transforms between frames. For a light pulse of energy $E$ moving along the $+x$ direction, the energy in the moving frame is

$E' = \gamma E(1 + v/c)$,

and for a pulse moving in the $-x$ direction

$E' = \gamma E(1 - v/c)$,

where $\gamma=1/\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}$. (These follow because light energy is proportional to frequency and the relativistic Doppler formula gives $\nu' = \gamma(1\pm v/c)\nu$.)

Apply this to the two pulses (each of energy $L/2$ in $S$):

\[ L' = \frac{L}{2}\gamma(1+v/c) + \frac{L}{2}\gamma(1-v/c) = \gamma L. \]

Step 3 — Subtract the two conservation relations

From $S$ we had $E_0 - E_1 = L$. From $S'$ we have $H_0 - H_1 = L' = \gamma L$. Subtract the first from the second:

\[ (H_0 - E_0) - (H_1 - E_1) = L(\gamma - 1). \]

Einstein then interpreted the combination $H - E$ as the kinetic energy of the body in the moving frame (up to an additive constant independent of the body's state). In modern terms, if a body's energy in its rest frame is $E$ and in a frame where it moves with velocity $v$ is $H$, then the difference $H-E$ equals the kinetic energy $K(v)$ plus a possible constant that cancels when taking differences.

Step 4 — Connect the difference to a change in kinetic energy

Let the body's masses before and after emission be $m_0$ and $m_1$, and denote their kinetic energies in the $S'$ frame as $K_0$ and $K_1$. Then (up to the same additive constant which cancels):

\[ (H_0 - E_0) - (H_1 - E_1) = K_0 - K_1. \]

For low speeds (small $v$) the nonrelativistic expression for kinetic energy is a good approximation:

\[ K \approx \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2. \]

Thus, to first order in $v^2$,

\[ K_0 - K_1 \approx \tfrac{1}{2}(m_0 - m_1)v^2. \]

Equate this with the right-hand side found earlier:

\[ \tfrac{1}{2}(m_0 - m_1)v^2 \;=\; L(\gamma - 1). \]

Step 5 — Expand $\gamma$ for small $v$ and take the limit

For small $v/c$, expand $\gamma$:

\[ \gamma = 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\frac{v^2}{c^2} + O\big(\tfrac{v^4}{c^4}\big). \]

So

\[ L(\gamma - 1) \approx L\cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\frac{v^2}{c^2}. \]

Plugging into the kinetic-energy relation gives

\[ \tfrac{1}{2}(m_0 - m_1)v^2 \approx L\cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\frac{v^2}{c^2}. \]

Cancel $\tfrac{1}{2}v^2$ (assume $v\neq 0$), yielding

\[ m_0 - m_1 = \frac{L}{c^2}. \]

Thus, when the body emits energy $L$ as radiation, its mass decreases by $L/c^2$. Interpreting this as a general correspondence between mass and energy leads to the conclusion that the energy content associated with mass $m$ (at rest) is

\[ E = mc^2. \]

Comments and modern viewpoint

  • Einstein's original argument used the thought experiment, energy conservation, and the Doppler transformation for light. He expanded to first order in $v^2/c^2$ and concluded the general result from the low-velocity limit.
  • Modern derivations typically use the four-momentum invariant or the relativistic energy formula $E=\gamma mc^2$ which give the result more compactly. But Einstein's 1905 paper is notable for its conceptual clarity and minimal assumptions.

References: Einstein, A. (1905). "Ist die Trägheit eines Körpers von seinem Energieinhalt abhängig?" Annalen der Physik. This page follows the logic of that short paper but uses modern notation for clarity.
